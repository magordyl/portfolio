Planner V1 · Design Explorer v3 · 2026-04-12

Six pairs. One European spring.

Six new directions after v2's muted pine read as too washed out and the amber celebration clashed. This round: saturated, alive primaries — led by a saturated European-spring beech canopy — each paired with a celebration accent in a different hue family so it pops without fighting. The frame is still locked (warm paper canvas, Fraunces × Inter × JetBrains Mono, dense mobile-first, 150–200ms motion). Only --accent and --celebration change per frame.

Why the pairings don't clash this time → v2 crashed because pine and amber were at similar saturations and close enough on the warm-cool axis to compete. The fix: primaries got more saturated (the brief permits it because celebration is sparing), and celebrations were pushed into genuinely different hue families — blossom pink for the spring green, citron for the cerulean, electric lime for the aubergine, etc. Warm–cool contrast where possible, complementary opposites where that reads as ceremonial rather than alarming.

How to read this round

Row 1 (01-03) — the required green and blue, plus an unexpected purple. These are the three high-saturation, high-contrast options. Each celebration hue lives in a different hue family to the primary so they pop without fighting.

Row 2 (04-06) — alternative moods. 04 Graphite × Azure is the most restrained (Things-like near-monochrome with one electric point of colour). 05 Teal × Apricot is the most versatile bridge between green and blue with the softest celebration. 06 Heather × Mint is the quietest pairing — both accents are botanical and calm, so the dopamine is gentler but always-on-brand.

Why these aren't clashing like v2. v2's pine and amber were at similar saturations and close enough on the warm-cool axis that they fought for attention. v3 fixes this by (a) pushing primaries more saturated so they stop reading as washed-out and (b) pushing celebrations into clearly different hue families — pink for green, citron (not amber) for blue, lime for purple, azure for near-black, apricot for teal, mint for plum. Most pairs are complementary or split-complementary, which creates ceremony not chaos.
