## Wes Kao: source posts analysed

**Storytelling focus (deep read):**
1. [Start right before you get eaten by the bear](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/start-right-before-you-get-eaten-by-the-bear). Cutting backstory.
2. [Technical leaders make these 3 common storytelling mistakes](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/technical-leaders-make-these-4-common). How technical framing hurts narrative.
3. [Playing defense: how to control the narrative if your work is being questioned](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/playing-defense-how-to-control-narrative). Credibility in live dialogue.
4. [Too detailed in the wrong ways, not detailed enough in the right ways](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/too-detailed-in-the-wrong-ways). Connective tissue between details.

**General style (sample):**
5. [How to be concise](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/how-to-be-concise). Her thinking on clarity.
6. [15 principles for managing up](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/15-principles-for-managing-up). Long-form listicle structure.
7. [Good design can obscure poor logic](https://newsletter.weskao.com/p/good-design-can-obscure-poor-logic). Substance over polish.

### What to steal from Kao

**1. Cold opens and governing metaphors.**
Every storytelling post I read opens with a concrete scene, not a definition. "Start right before you get eaten by the bear" is the title, the opening, and the touchstone she returns to four times in the piece. The metaphor *is* the argument.

> "When I'm explaining a situation, I remind myself to 'start right before you get eaten by the bear.'"

She never introduces the concept academically. She uses it, then lets the reader infer the frame. Compare that to the usual "In this post, I'll cover..." scaffolding that signals the author isn't sure the reader will stick around.

**2. Compression through direct imperative.**
Kao trusts imperatives more than explanations. Examples that jumped out:
- "When you think there's nothing left to cut, cut some more."
- "Ditch the cascade." (actually Cutler, but same move)
- "Read that again."
- "Seriously, do not attempt."

No hedging, no "you might consider." The imperative lands harder and leaves the reader with less to resist. Use for lessons, warnings, and anything you want the reader to actually do.

**3. Self-deprecation as credibility.**
Kao opens "How to be concise" with "I'm not naturally a concise person." She admits susceptibility to the very problem she's diagnosing. This is surprisingly persuasive. It disarms the reader's "easy for you to say" reflex before they can raise it.

Applied to case studies: admit what didn't work, what you got wrong the first time, what took longer than expected. Don't smooth it over. The honest stumble is the credibility.

**4. Permission-granting language.**
"You'd be surprised at how little backstory is needed" / "It's okay to be brief" / "No one will miss the setup." Kao repeatedly gives the reader permission to do the hard thing. This reframes conciseness from "what I'm taking away" to "what I'm releasing you from."

**5. Connective tissue between details.**
From "Too detailed in the wrong ways": details without connective tissue are waste. Numbers without narrative force cognitive load with no payoff. Always state *why* a detail matters before or immediately after dropping it.

**6. Structural patterns she uses repeatedly:**
- **Problem → correction → tactics.** Debunk misconception, then identify the root cause, then give actionable fixes. ("How to be concise" is the cleanest example.)
- **Anecdote → principle → applications.** Open with a personal story, extract one principle, then list places it applies.
- **Numbered principles with opening hooks.** Each principle begins with a directive sentence, never "This principle is about..."

### What NOT to steal from Kao

- **The emoji icon sets** (🚫 ✅ etc.) feel very LinkedIn. Dylan's voice doesn't use emoji and shouldn't start now.
- **The "at first glance, it seems simple enough, right?" conversational wind-up.** She uses it well. It reads as chatty for a technical portfolio.
- **Authority citations to management theory (Andy Grove, Paul Graham).** Borrowed authority is a common move, but Dylan's portfolio is a product-thinking portfolio, not a management-coaching newsletter. The move reads as name-dropping if the audience isn't already primed.
- **Selling a course at the end of every post.** Obvious, flagged for completeness.

## John Cutler: source posts analysed

Top-ranked posts by engagement on Substack archive:

1. [TBM 229: Winnable and Unwinnable Games (Part 1)](https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-229-winnable-and-unwinnable-games). 343 likes.

4. [TBM 240: Developer Counter-Productivity](https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-240-the-ultimate-guide-to-developer). 171 likes.
5. [TBM 41/51: Why Goal Cascades Are Harmful](https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-4151-why-goal-cascades-are-harmful). 160 likes.
6. [TBM 279: How to Learn and Practice PM in a Feature Factory](https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-279-how-to-learn-and-practice). 133 likes.

### Cutler's use of illustrations: the main reason we care

This is the dimension Dylan specifically called out. Here's what I found.

**1. Diagrams replace prose, they don't supplement it.**
In TBM 229 (Winnable Games), three diagrams do the work of around 12 paragraphs of text:
- A system-dynamics diagram showing interconnected organisational pressures.
- A driver/constraint/float diagram showing how too many drivers plus insufficient floats equals gridlock.
- A causal loop diagram showing the self-reinforcing cycle of low-trust dysfunction.

Each one *replaces* what would otherwise require a lengthy explanation. Cutler's prose around the diagram is short: a setup sentence, the diagram, then a one-line takeaway. The diagram is load-bearing.

**2. Diagrams appear BEFORE the explanation, not after.**
This is the single most distinctive move. In "Why Goal Cascades Are Harmful":
- Chaotic 24-goal network diagram, then text about messy reality.
- Clean cascade pyramid, then critique of its "seductive simplicity."
- Alternative flowchart, then actionable steps.

The diagrams prime visual pattern recognition *before* language names the pattern. Readers see the thing, feel the tension, then get language to describe what they already sensed.

**3. Style: hand-sketch, schematic, never polished.**
All Cutler diagrams are raw: rough boxes, arrows drawn with wobble, handwriting-adjacent labels. They look like whiteboard photos, not Figma exports. The aesthetic signals "I drew this to think, not to decorate," which is the whole point. Polished diagrams trigger Kao's "good design obscures poor logic" problem. Cutler's sketches can't hide weak thinking.

**Conventions worth adopting for portfolio case study diagrams:**
- Linework style: sketch or hand-drawn, not vector-perfect.
- Colour: monochrome or Royal Tonal primary only. No rainbow taxonomies.
- Typography: sans, matching body (Geist). Handwriting fonts would look affected.
- Caption: one short sentence explaining what the diagram shows, never what it means. Let the prose extract the meaning.
- Placement: always before the paragraph that interprets it.

**4. Four diagram types Cutler uses repeatedly:**
- **System-dynamics or causal loop.** For self-reinforcing cycles (why bad states are sticky).
- **2×2 matrices.** For separating concerns (TBM 245 uses these to decouple urgency from value from duration).
- **Flowcharts or process diagrams.** For sequences (prioritisation workshop, alternative to cascades).
- **Network or constellation diagrams.** For "here's the messy reality" (24 goals, dependency webs).

### Cutler's structural moves for complex messages

**1. Steelman before you critique.**
In "Why Goal Cascades Are Harmful," Cutler opens by acknowledging cascades feel "wonderfully symmetrical." He names the *appeal* of the thing he's about to dismantle. Readers who bought into cascades don't feel attacked. They feel understood. The critique lands harder because the setup wasn't strawman.

> Rule: before dismantling any approach, state what makes it attractive. The bug is usually in the appeal, not despite it.

**2. Name and label concepts.**
Cutler coins or adopts terms aggressively: "skilled pragmatists," "winnable games," "drivers and floats," "spidey sense," "promotion-driven development." Each label gives the reader a hook to recall and reference the idea later.

For case studies: if a trade-off comes up multiple times, give it a name. "The [X] problem," "the [Y] tax," or "the [Z] tension." Named concepts travel; unnamed ones don't.

**3. Taxonomy as scaffolding for nuance.**
TBM 229 uses a 12-item taxonomy of "what makes a good game," then a 5-item taxonomy of "responses to low-trust environments." Numbered taxonomies let Cutler cover nuance without feeling diffuse. Each item is its own small argument.

Works especially well when an idea has more than 3 dimensions and you don't want to flatten them.

**4. Actionable ending over philosophical ending.**
Every post I read ends with imperative instructions, not open questions. Even "Winnable Games Part 1," which explicitly defers solutions to Part 2, ends by naming three concrete meta-questions readers can hold. Not a fuzzy "what do you think?"

> Cutler rule: never end a post by asking the reader to reflect. End by asking them to *do*. Reflection happens in the middle; action happens at the close.

### Cutler's relatability moves

- **Imagined monologue from a named character.** "Maria is a skilled pragmatist... 'I have my projects outside of work to be challenged; thank you very much.'" Characters make abstract patterns concrete without generalising "people do X."
- **Acknowledging privilege and scale.** "Skilled pragmatists often have the privilege of being ABLE to detach." He refuses to flatten the reader base. He notes that the advice applies unequally.
- **Direct validation of reader frustration.** "You will not get fired" (repeated 4 times in TBM 279). Addresses the fear by name and repetition.
- **Vulnerability about his own limits.** "I always remind managers that they may not have the full picture, even if they've worked at some of the top companies in the world." He implies himself in the "may not have the full picture" bucket.
- **Humour through inversion.** "Developer productivity is so August 2023. Enter Developer Counter-Productivity." The title itself is a joke at the expense of the consultant-economy narrative.

### What NOT to steal from Cutler

- **The TBM episode numbering.** It's his newsletter format. Imitating it would be cargo-cult.
- **The frequency-based publishing cadence.** He posts a lot. Dylan's portfolio writing should be sparse and load-bearing. Every post earns its place.
- **Consultant-style "here's a framework for X" as the default shape.** Cutler writes for a PM audience that expects frameworks. Dylan's audience (hiring managers, peers, curious readers) benefits more from lived stories than framework extraction. Use his *techniques* (taxonomy, labelling, sketches) but not his *default shape*.

### AI tells to explicitly avoid

These are the signals that make a reader feel they're reading machine-generated text rather than a person. The style guide should treat all of these as prohibited unless there's a specific reason to override.

**Punctuation and connective tells:**
- The em-dash (`—`) as an aside connective. This is the single most recognisable tell. Use full stops, commas, parentheses, or a new sentence instead.
- The en-dash (`–`) used the same way as an em-dash.
- "Not just X, but Y" parallel constructions.
- "It's not about X; it's about Y" balanced oppositions.
- Triads with parallel structure ("clear, concise, and actionable").

**Vocabulary tells:**
- "Delve," "tapestry," "realm," "landscape," "journey," "unpacks," "resonates."
- "Navigate" as a verb for handling a situation (e.g. "navigate the complexities").
- "Robust," "seamless," "elevate," "vibrant," "nuanced," "multifaceted."
- "Essentially," "fundamentally," "ultimately" as sentence qualifiers.
- "It's worth noting that," "it's important to recognise that."
- "Bridging the gap."
- "In essence," "at its core."

**Structural tells:**
- Opening every paragraph with a topic sentence that previews what's coming.
- Closing paragraphs with a restatement of what was just said.
- "Here's the thing:" as a transition.
- "Let me explain..." or "I'll walk you through..." meta-commentary.
- Headers shaped as "Title: Subtitle" (colon followed by a reframe).
- Over-even paragraph lengths (suspiciously balanced blocks).
- Numbered "Here are five reasons..." framings when prose would do.

**Tonal tells:**
- Breathless enthusiasm: "amazing," "exciting," "incredible," "thrilled."
- Hedging softeners: "might just be," "could well be," "tends to."
- False humility: "I'd argue that," "one could say."
- Sentence-initial "Importantly,".
- Perfect topic-sentence / evidence / conclusion paragraph rhythm, every paragraph.

**What to do instead:**
The style guidance section of the style guide will capture the positive rules as Dylan's voice emerges from chunk 4d. For now, the operating instruction is: **when uncertain, choose the option that sounds less polished.** Rough edges, uneven paragraph lengths, occasional fragments, and a willingness to end a section abruptly are all counter-signals to AI output.

### Durable preferences already established

From Dylan's conversational record (which is genuinely his, even if compressed):

- **Australian spellings throughout.** favourite, organised, analyse, colour, realise, behaviour. Never US spellings.
- **No emoji anywhere.**
- **No exclamation marks.**
- **Terse over effusive.** "Right call" rather than "That's a fantastic point."
- **Declarative claims.** "This works" rather than "This might work" or "I'd argue this works."
- **Decisions marked explicitly.** When a decision is being made or noted, say so in plain language.

These are the only style-level rules that survive chunk 4a. Everything else is discovered in 4d.

## Synthesis preview (for step 4a.4)

The final style guide (`.claude/rules/writing-style.md`) will split into two clearly labelled sections.

### Part 1: Structural guidance (locked in 4a)

Structure is transferable between writers, so this section can be fully defined in 4a based on the Kao and Cutler research. Expected contents:

1. **Opening: default to a problem/opportunity hook; claim-first is the alternative.** From the user's decision: "they should probably start with a hook that includes the problem/opportunity that I noticed. Both options available for case studies."
2. **Steelman any approach before critiquing it.** Cutler rule. State what makes the wrong answer attractive before explaining why it's wrong.
3. **Name and label recurring tensions.** Cutler rule. If a trade-off comes up more than once in a piece, give it a short label and reuse it.
4. **Stakes and trade-offs stated inline, never buried.** "This buys X but costs Y" as the basic shape for decisions.
5. **Diagrams placed before the paragraph that interprets them.** Cutler rule. Visual pattern recognition primes comprehension.
6. **Four diagram archetypes.** System-dynamics or causal loop; 2×2 matrix; flowchart; network or constellation. Pick one. Don't invent new ones.
7. **Imperative endings.** Close with what the reader can do or take, not with a question or reflection prompt.
8. **Connective tissue for details.** Kao rule. Never drop a number, quote, or fact without stating why it matters.
9. **Taxonomies for nuance.** Numbered lists work better than flowing prose when an idea has more than three dimensions.
10. **Self-deprecating honesty in Lessons sections.** What didn't work is the credibility layer.
